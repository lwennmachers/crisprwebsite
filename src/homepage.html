About CRISPR

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a groundbreaking gene-editing tool inspired by bacterial defense mechanisms. Bacteria use CRISPR to protect themselves from viruses by storing viral DNA and targeting it with precision.

At the heart of CRISPR technology is the Cas9 protein, which acts like molecular scissors to cut DNA. This process allows scientists to add, remove, or replace specific genetic sequences.

Key DNA Features

  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): The molecule that carries genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all living organisms.
  • Genes: Segments of DNA that code for proteins, which perform most of the functions in the body.
  • Guide RNA (gRNA): A synthetic RNA sequence designed to match a target DNA sequence. It guides the Cas9 protein to the exact location in the DNA to make a cut.
  • Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM): A short DNA sequence that is required for Cas9 to bind to the target DNA. It acts as a recognition signal.
